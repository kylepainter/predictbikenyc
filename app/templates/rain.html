Biking in the rain

Bikes are a bit faster during rain than during sunny weather. While not a large difference (.11 mph), it is a statistically significant gap given that these are averages of 5 million+ sunny rides and nearly 200,000 rainy rides.




Why is this? Does rain cause cyclists to ride faster, or are there diffferent cyclists out on rainy days?







The data gives us a few clues. Rainy cyclists are half a year younger and 16% more male than during sunny times.







We can find an expected average speed by multiplying the age distribution of riders by the average speed of each age. The expected sunny-day speed for this group of cyclists is slightly lower than their actual speed during the rain.





Conclusion: age/gender cohort explains only 52% of the difference between sunny and rainy weather. The rest of the difference is likely dependent on self-selection of riders and higher motivation to finish the trip quickly.
